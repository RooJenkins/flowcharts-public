Follow the Deal

Verification Engine

January 2026 · Full Specification

Follow the Deal provides practical legal guidance for renewable energy projects in emerging markets. Because this content will be generated or assisted by AI, Follow the Deal employs a rigorous multi-stage verification system designed to catch errors, outdated information, and AI hallucinations before they reach users.

Every piece of legal content is independently verified against authoritative sources, cross-checked for internal consistency, and where NZLA requires legal review, reviewed by contributing lawyers before publication. No content is published on trust alone. Everything must be provable.
Why This Matters
AI language models can occasionally generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information: misremembered laws, confused organisations, inaccurate costs, or outdated processes. In legal guidance, even rare errors could cause real harm to project developers, investors, and communities. The verification system ensures these errors are caught before publication.

AI Error Patterns in Legal Content

When AI does produce errors, they tend to follow predictable patterns. The system specifically targets these:

Incorrect Legislation
AI may produce law references that follow correct formatting but cite laws that don't exist or are outdated
Confused Organisations
AI may confuse or misname government agencies, programs, or bodies
Unverified Numbers
AI may produce costs, timelines, or statistics that need verification against authoritative sources
Orphan Citations
AI may cite sources that have moved, changed, or don't contain the claimed information
Confident Errors
AI may state incorrect information without flagging uncertainty, making errors harder to spot
Internal Contradictions
AI may produce inconsistencies within a document: different timelines, costs, or requirements in different places

Content Generation

Verification catches errors, but the best approach is to reduce errors at the source. Follow the Deal uses structured generation practices that make hallucinations less likely in the first place.

Focused Context Windows

AI quality degrades when context becomes cluttered or contradictory ("context rot"). Each generation task receives only the information it needs: the specific checkpoint being written, relevant legislation for that jurisdiction, and applicable templates. No extraneous content that could confuse or contaminate the output.

↓
Source-Grounded Generation

Content is never generated from the AI's general knowledge alone. Every generation task includes authoritative source documents: official gazette extracts, government ministry publications, verified legal texts. The AI synthesises and structures this material rather than inventing from scratch.

↓
Structured Output Templates

Content follows consistent templates that require specific, verifiable information in defined fields. "Estimated timeline: [X] days" forces a concrete answer that can be checked, rather than vague language that sounds plausible but says nothing. Templates also ensure completeness: missing sections are obvious.

↓
Checkpoint Segmentation

Complex topics are broken into discrete checkpoints, each generated independently. This prevents the AI from losing track of details across a long document and makes inconsistencies between sections easier to detect. Each checkpoint is small enough to verify thoroughly.

↓
Citation-Required Generation

The AI is instructed to cite sources for every factual claim during generation, not just provide citations for review later. Claims without sources are flagged immediately. This shifts the burden: unsourced statements are treated as incomplete rather than accepted by default.

18-Stage Verification Engine

Every piece of content passes through 18 verification stages. Each stage targets specific types of errors. Content earns points for passing checks, and scores determine whether content can be published, needs review, or must be blocked.

Phase 1: Source & Link Verification

1. URL Accessibility Check

Every URL referenced in the content is tested. The system follows redirects and confirms pages actually load. Dead links, timeouts, and broken redirects are flagged.

↓
2. Citation Content Verification Error Prevention

Just because a URL works doesn't mean the citation is accurate. This stage downloads the actual content of each cited page and verifies it contains the information claimed.

Uses both keyword matching and semantic similarity analysis to catch valid paraphrasing while detecting misattribution. If a citation claims "the permit takes 30 days" but the source says "45 days," this stage catches it.

Falls back to archive.org for dead links to recover historical content.

↓
3. Source Authority Validation

Every factual claim must trace back to an authoritative source. The system maintains a registry of trusted sources for each jurisdiction.

Accepted: Government ministry websites, official .gov domains, regulatory bodies, development finance institutions (World Bank, AfDB, IFC, EBRD), peer-reviewed sources.

Rejected: Wikipedia, forums, social media, unverifiable sources.

↓
4. Legal Concept Mapping Error Prevention

Translation is not enough—legal concepts must map correctly. When working across jurisdictions and languages, the system verifies that translated terms correspond to equivalent legal concepts, not just linguistic equivalents.

Key distinctions verified:

  • "Concession" (Civil Law) vs "Lease" (Common Law)—different liability structures
  • "Autorisation" vs "Permis" vs "Licence"—different administrative weight
  • "Force majeure" definitions vary significantly across legal systems
  • Land tenure concepts (usufruct, emphyteusis) have no direct Common Law equivalents

Content using cross-jurisdictional legal concepts is flagged for review by lawyers familiar with both legal traditions.

Phase 2: Legal Reference Verification

5. Legislation Existence Check Error Prevention

This is a primary defence against AI errors. AI can sometimes produce legislation references that follow correct formatting conventions but cite laws that don't exist or are outdated.

For every legislation reference, the system queries official government databases:

Mozambique: Boletim da República (Lei, Decreto, Resolução formats)
Morocco: Bulletin Officiel (Dahir, Décret formats in Arabic and French)
Vietnam: Official Gazette (Luật, Nghị định, Thông tư)
International: EUR-Lex for EU law, UNFCCC databases, treaty repositories

If a referenced law cannot be found in the official database, it is flagged as a potential error and blocked pending human verification.

↓
6. Legislation Currency Check

Confirms that cited legislation is still in force and hasn't been repealed, superseded, or significantly amended. A correctly-cited law from 2018 that was repealed in 2023 is just as dangerous as a fabricated law.

The system tracks amendment histories and flags content citing superseded legislation for immediate review.

↓
7. Multi-Source Confirmation High-Risk Content

For high-risk content (binding legal requirements, safety information, regulatory deadlines), verification against a single source is not sufficient. The system requires confirmation from 2-3 independent sources.

"Independent" means different domains and ideally different publication dates. Two pages from the same ministry website don't count as independent confirmation.

If sources conflict (one says 28 days, another says 42 days), the content is blocked and flagged for human resolution rather than guessing.

↓
8. Normative Hierarchy Check Error Prevention

A law may be valid but superseded. Legislation can be "current" (not formally repealed) yet effectively overridden by newer, more specific laws. The system checks for conflict of laws situations.

Hierarchy rules applied:

  • Lex specialis derogat legi generali—specific laws override general ones (Investment Code trumps General Tax Decree)
  • Lex posterior derogat legi priori—newer laws override older ones on the same subject
  • Constitutional provisions override ordinary legislation
  • Ratified international treaties may override domestic law

When potentially conflicting laws apply to the same situation, the system flags the conflict for legal review rather than defaulting to one interpretation.

Phase 3: Entity & Contact Verification

9. Organisation Existence Check Error Prevention

AI can sometimes confuse or misname organisations. Every named organisation, government agency, regulatory body, or program mentioned in the content must be verified to actually exist.

The system maintains a registry of known entities per jurisdiction and searches official government websites to confirm any new entities. References to organisations that cannot be verified are flagged for review.

Example catches: "Ministry of Renewable Energy Development" (doesn't exist in that form), "National Solar Registration Authority" (confused with another body), "Green Energy Fast-Track Program" (incorrect name).

↓
10. Contact Information Verification Error Prevention

Phone numbers, email addresses, physical addresses, and website URLs for organisations must be verified against official sources. AI can sometimes produce incorrect or outdated contact details.

Verification methods:

  • Phone numbers checked against official government directories
  • Email domains verified to match official organisation domains
  • Physical addresses validated against known office locations
  • Website URLs tested for accessibility and correct ownership

Phase 4: Numerical & Cost Verification

11. Numerical Grounding Check Error Prevention

Every number must have a source. AI can sometimes produce specific figures ("$4,847.23", "exactly 47 days", "23.7% reduction") that sound authoritative but may not be accurate.

Numbers without traceable sources are flagged. Unusually precise figures are flagged for verification.

↓
12. Cost Range Validation

Cost estimates are checked against known market ranges for each jurisdiction. The system maintains benchmarks for renewable energy project costs, regulatory fees, and professional services by country and project type. Implausible figures are flagged.

↓
13. De Facto Reality Check Market Reality

What the law says vs what actually happens. In emerging markets, official timelines and processes often differ significantly from practice. A legally mandated "30-day permit" may routinely take 6 months due to administrative backlogs, informal requirements, or capacity constraints.

Reality checks performed:

  • Official timelines compared against market practitioner reports
  • Stated costs verified against recent transaction data where available
  • Process steps checked against practitioner experience (informal requirements)
  • Capacity constraints flagged (e.g., single-person approval bottlenecks)

When significant gaps exist between de jure (legal) and de facto (practical) reality, content includes both perspectives with clear labelling: "Legal requirement: 30 days. Typical practice: 4-6 months."

Phase 5: Consistency & Logic Verification

14. Internal Consistency Check Error Prevention

AI can sometimes produce inconsistencies. The system scans for internal contradictions across the entire checkpoint:

Checks performed:

  • Same timeline mentioned consistently (not "30 days" in one place, "6 weeks" elsewhere)
  • Same costs quoted consistently throughout
  • Contact information matches for the same organisation
  • Process steps don't contradict each other
  • No circular dependencies (Step A requires Step B, Step B requires Step A)

↓
15. Cross-Checkpoint Consistency

Beyond internal consistency, the system checks for contradictions across related checkpoints. If Checkpoint A says the energy regulator is "ARENE" but Checkpoint B says it's "ARNE," that inconsistency is flagged.

This catches cases where AI generates internally-consistent but mutually-contradictory content across different sections of the platform.

↓
16. Temporal Logic Validation

Checks that timelines and dates make logical sense:

  • No references to future dates as if they've already occurred
  • Historical dates verified (law passage dates, policy implementation dates)
  • Process timelines are realistic and don't contain impossibilities
  • Content freshness assessed (cost data from 2020 may be outdated by 2026)

Phase 6: Pattern-Based Error Detection

17. Error Pattern Matching Error Prevention

AI errors tend to follow detectable patterns. This stage applies pattern-based detection rules learned from known AI failure modes:

Patterns flagged:

  • Legislation references following correct format but not existing in databases
  • Organisations with plausible names but no online presence
  • Contact details that look correct but lead nowhere
  • Suspiciously round numbers mixed with suspiciously specific ones
  • "Orphan citations": properly formatted references that can't be verified
  • Claims presented with high confidence but no supporting evidence

Multiple patterns in the same content increase the risk score.

↓
18. Negative Claim Verification Human Required

Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Claims that something is not required are inherently harder to verify than claims that something is required. AI cannot reliably prove a negative.

High-risk negative claims that trigger mandatory human review:

  • "No permit/licence/authorisation is required"
  • "Tax exempt" or "no tax applies"
  • "No environmental assessment needed"
  • "No local content requirements"
  • "No community consultation required"
  • Any claim of regulatory exemption or exception

These claims cannot be verified by confirming a source exists—they require confirmation that no contradicting requirement exists, which demands exhaustive legal research. All high-risk negative claims are blocked pending expert legal review.

Ongoing Monitoring

Post-Publication Monitoring

Verification doesn't stop at publication. The system continuously monitors published content for staleness:

  • Cited legislation checked for amendments or repeal
  • Source URLs monitored for significant changes
  • Cost data flagged for review after age thresholds
  • Regulatory changes in target jurisdictions trigger content review

When changes are detected, affected content is flagged for re-verification and content owners are notified.

What This Means for Users

When you use Follow the Deal guidance:

Every legal reference has been verified against official government databases. Every organisation mentioned has been confirmed to exist. Every cost estimate has been checked for plausibility. Every URL has been tested. Every citation has been verified to actually say what is claimed.

Content that couldn't meet these standards either includes appropriate disclaimers or was blocked from publication entirely.
